4.1 Content Ownership, IP Management & Legal Considerations

  • Parallax B2C Model:
    • Platform IP (Original Templates): Document your creative process (concept, prompting, curation, editing) for Parallax horror short templates. Assert copyright on your specific templates, even when AI-generated.
    • User-Uploaded Content: Terms of Service (ToS) must clarify that users own or have rights to uploaded selfies, and grant Parallax a license to use only for creating the face-swapped video.
    • Face-Swapped Video Output: User receives a personal, non-commercial license to the video. Underlying template IP remains yours. ToS should state this clearly. Commercial rights only with specific agreement.
  • Kubrik B2B & B2C:
    • B2B (Studios/Production Houses): Typically, the studio owns the film, you retain IP in the Kubrik tech/platform.
    • B2C (Individual Creators): Users might receive personal-use licenses. If a user prompt leads to a wholly unique film, copyright is still murky under US law—significant human authorship is required. ToS must reflect this evolving landscape.
  • AI-Generated Content: Copyright Landscape (Early 2025):
    • US Copyright Office: Pure AI works (no human authorship) not protected. Claim copyright by demonstrating substantial human creative input (prompting, selection, arrangement, curation, editing).
    • Be aware of AI tool training data issues—use platforms with clear licensing or your own data if possible.
    • Best practice: Document your human involvement at every stage. Use AI as an assistant, not sole creator, for anything you wish to copyright. Stay current on legal shifts.
    • Consult IP/AI legal counsel before commercial launch.
  • User Agreements & ToS for Parallax:
    • Clear on selfie/image rights, license to Parallax, template IP, output licenses, prohibited uses (no deepfakes, illegal/infringing content).
    • Disclaimer of warranties/limitation of liability. Simple privacy policy. Use a reputable ToS generator for MVP; review with legal counsel before scaling.

4.3 Future Scalability & Adapting to AI Advancements

  • Design for Evolving AI:
    • Kubrik’s modular architecture allows individual modules (Iris, Monolith, etc.) to be upgraded/replaced as tech evolves—no need to rebuild the whole system for each advancement.
    • Define (even conceptually) each module’s inputs and outputs, enabling easier upgrades and integration.
    • Keep logic and creative workflows “tool agnostic” so the system remains robust as new tools appear.
  • Continuous Research & Integration Process:
    • Monthly/quarterly research to track new AI tools (prompting, video, audio, editing).
    • Sandbox testing before mainline adoption. Evaluate for output quality, control, API, cost, performance, and alignment with style guide.
    • Phased integration: update one module at a time, document new workflows and impacts.
  • Long-Term Autonomy Vision:

5.1 Appendix A: Glossary of AI & Film Terms

  • LLM: Large Language Model. An advanced AI model trained on vast datasets to generate and manipulate text (e.g., GPT-4, Claude 3).
  • Prompt Engineering: The process of crafting inputs to get desired outputs from AI models.
  • GenAI: Generative AI—refers to models/tools that create new content (video, audio, text, images) from prompts.
  • MVP: Minimum Viable Product. The simplest version of a product that can be launched to test the core idea with users.
  • Concierge MVP: An MVP where some “automated” functions are performed manually behind the scenes to test and validate product/market fit quickly.
  • EDL: Edit Decision List. A file used by video editors to describe a sequence of edits for post-production workflows.
  • Asset Management System (AMS): A tool or database for organizing and tracking digital media assets (scripts, video, audio, images, metadata).

5.4 Appendix D: Kubrik Style Guide & Ruleset (Sample Excerpt)

  • Narrative Themes: Reality distortion, paranoia, technology as both tool and threat, personal vs. cosmic horror.
  • Visual Style: Muted color palette, deep shadows, symmetrical compositions, recurring motifs (mirrors, static shots, VHS textures).
  • Pacing: Slow build, tension before release, minimal jump scares (earned only), frequent use of long, unbroken takes.
  • Sound: Minimalist score, sudden diegetic sound cues, heavy reverb, “empty” space.
